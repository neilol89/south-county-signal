## Business Entity

### Sole Proprietorship (Simplest Start)
You can launch the newsletter as a sole proprietorship under your own name or a DBA ("doing business as") filing for "The South County Signal." This requires minimal paperwork — just a DBA filing with the Rhode Island Secretary of State if you're using a business name.

### LLC (Recommended When Revenue Begins)
Once the newsletter generates revenue, forming an LLC provides liability protection and looks more professional to advertisers. Rhode Island LLC formation is straightforward:

- File Articles of Organization with RI Secretary of State
- Filing fee: ~$150
- Annual report fee: $50/year
- Operating agreement (single-member LLC — can be simple)
- Get an EIN from the IRS (free, online)
- Open a business bank account

Since you're already evaluating an LLC for another venture in Rhode Island, it may make sense to house the newsletter under the same entity or create a separate one depending on liability considerations. An attorney can advise on the cleanest structure.

## CAN-SPAM Compliance

The CAN-SPAM Act governs commercial email in the United States. Key requirements:

1. **Don't use deceptive headers.** Your "From" name and email must accurately identify you or your business.
2. **Don't use deceptive subject lines.** The subject must relate to the content of the email.
3. **Identify the message as an ad** if the primary purpose is commercial. For a newsletter with mixed editorial and advertising, the FTC looks at the overall character — as long as editorial content is primary, you're fine. Sponsored sections should be labeled.
4. **Include your physical address.** This can be a PO Box. Include it in the footer.
5. **Provide an unsubscribe mechanism.** Beehiiv handles this automatically.
6. **Honor opt-out requests within 10 business days.** Beehiiv handles this automatically.
7. **Monitor what third parties do on your behalf.** If you hire a freelancer or VA who sends emails for you, you're still responsible.

**Penalties:** Up to $51,744 per email sent in violation. Take this seriously — but Beehiiv's built-in compliance tools handle most of it.

## FTC Disclosure for Sponsored Content

The Federal Trade Commission requires clear and conspicuous disclosure of material connections between advertisers and endorsers. For the newsletter:

### What Must Be Disclosed
- Any section where a business paid for placement
- Any section where you received free products/services in exchange for coverage
- Affiliate links where you earn a commission

### How to Disclose

**Headliner Sponsor:** "Presented by [Business Name]" tag clearly visible above the content. This is adequate disclosure.

**Featured Lineup Event:** "Featured" badge on the category tag, visually distinct from editorial picks. Adequate if the reader can distinguish paid from editorial at a glance.

**Food & Drink Spotlight (paid):** Add "Partner Spotlight" or "Sponsored" above the section when it's a paid placement. When it's an editorial pick (no payment), no label needed.

**Mid-Newsletter Ad:** "Our Sponsor" label. Clear and adequate.

**Insider Deal:** Inherently understood as a commercial arrangement. No additional disclosure needed beyond the existing deal format.

**Affiliate Links:** If you link to Eventbrite or other platforms with affiliate tracking, include a general disclosure in your footer: "Some links in this newsletter may earn us a small commission at no cost to you."

### Disclosure Don'ts
- Don't bury the disclosure at the bottom of the email
- Don't use vague language like "thanks to our friends at..."
- Don't rely on the reader knowing it's an ad — if it's paid, label it
- Don't use tiny font or low-contrast text for disclosure labels

## Copyright & Content Usage

### Your Content
Everything you write for the newsletter is your original work and your copyright. If you hire freelance writers, ensure your agreement specifies work-for-hire or assigns copyright to you.

### Event Information
Facts about events (dates, times, locations) are not copyrightable. You can freely list and describe any public event. However:

- Don't copy event descriptions verbatim from other sources
- Don't reproduce promotional images without permission (use your own photos or Canva graphics)
- Don't copy other newsletters' curation or writing

### Venue Photos
- Photos you take yourself: you own them, use freely
- Photos from venue websites/social media: ask permission or link to the original
- Stock photos: use Canva's library (license included with Canva Pro) or Unsplash (free, Creative Commons)

## Privacy & Data

### Subscriber Data
You collect email addresses and potentially names, towns, and interest preferences. This is personal data subject to privacy regulations.

**Best practices:**
- Only collect data you actually use
- Don't sell or share subscriber lists with third parties
- Include a privacy policy on your website (Beehiiv can host this)
- If an advertiser asks for your subscriber list, the answer is always NO — you sell placements, not data

### Privacy Policy (Required)
Your website needs a privacy policy. It should cover:
- What data you collect (email, name, custom fields)
- How you use it (to send the newsletter, personalize content)
- Third parties who process it (Beehiiv as your email service provider)
- How subscribers can opt out or request data deletion
- Your contact information

Beehiiv provides a basic privacy policy template. Customize it for your use case.

### GDPR Note
If you have subscribers from the EU (unlikely for a hyper-local South County newsletter, but possible), GDPR requires explicit consent and additional protections. Beehiiv's double opt-in feature helps with this. For a local newsletter, this is low risk but worth being aware of.

## Insurance

### General Liability
Not strictly required for a newsletter business, but consider a general liability policy ($500K–$1M) once revenue justifies it. This protects against claims like:
- A business claims your review harmed them
- Someone is injured at an event you promoted
- Copyright infringement claims

**Cost:** ~$300–500/year for a basic policy through a provider like Hiscox or Next Insurance.

### Errors & Omissions (E&O)
If you start offering advertising services or consulting as an add-on, E&O insurance protects against claims of professional negligence.

## Intellectual Property

### Trademark
Consider trademarking "The South County Signal" once the business is established and generating revenue. A federal trademark registration provides nationwide protection.

- USPTO filing fee: ~$250–350 per class
- Process: 8–12 months
- You can use ™ immediately (indicates you claim trademark rights); ® only after registration is granted

### Domain Protection
Register variations to prevent squatters:
- southcountysignal.com (primary)
- thesouthcountysignal.com (redirect)
- southcountysignal.net (defensive)
