## Voice & Tone

### Who We Sound Like
Your most plugged-in friend who lives in South County year-round — not just summer. They know every bar, every restaurant, every hidden beach access point. They're smart but not pretentious. They have opinions but aren't preachy. They'll recommend a $200 tasting menu and a $5 dive bar burger in the same email.

### Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What it means | What it doesn't mean |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Warm** | Welcoming, inclusive, like talking to a friend | Saccharine, corporate-friendly, generic |
| **Confident** | We know our stuff, we commit to recommendations | Arrogant, dismissive, gatekeeping |
| **Irreverent** | We have humor, we poke fun, we're not the chamber of commerce | Mean-spirited, snarky for no reason, alienating |
| **Honest** | We'll skip a section if nothing's good enough | Negative, critical of local businesses publicly |
| **Local** | We know the inside jokes, the back roads, the real names | Exclusionary, insider-y to the point of confusion |

### Tone by Context

**Newsletter opener:** Casual, breezy, seasonal. Like a text from a friend. *"The ocean hit 68° this week and the Gansett parking lots are filling up by 9am again."*

**Event descriptions:** Punchy, opinionated, useful. Lead with why someone should care, not logistics. *"Three-piece blues band with a loyal local following. Get there early — the Mist doesn't do reservations."*

**Spotlight/reviews:** Warm, specific, generous. We only feature places we'd actually go. *"The tuna poke bowl is absurdly good for a place most people write off as touristy."*

**Signal Noise (hot takes):** Playful, provocative, community-building. *"Best lobster roll in South County — and don't just say Aunt Carrie's because your parents did."*

**Advertiser communications:** Professional but still human. Not corporate-speak. We're a small business talking to small businesses.

### Words We Use
- "South County" (never "Washington County" unless legal context)
- "Gansett" (local shorthand for Narragansett)
- "The Mist" (for Ocean Mist — locals know)
- "Worth the drive" (for Providence picks)
- "This week's move" (our headline pick)
- "The lineup" (our event roundup)

### Words We Avoid
- "Exciting" (show, don't tell)
- "Amazing" / "incredible" (overused, says nothing)
- "Don't miss" (let the description sell it)
- "Dear readers" or "Hey subscribers" (too formal/mass-market)
- "Content" (when talking about our newsletter publicly — call it "the Signal" or "this week's issue")
- Any corporate newsletter language ("We're thrilled to announce...")

### Writing Rules
1. **Lead with the hook, not the name.** Don't start event descriptions with the event name. Start with why someone should care.
2. **One sentence can be a paragraph.** Especially in the opener. Short hits hard.
3. **Use em dashes — not semicolons.** They're more conversational.
4. **Contractions always.** "Don't" not "do not." "It's" not "it is."
5. **Address the reader as "you."** Never "our readers" or "subscribers."
6. **Opinions are allowed.** "The Bloody Mary is legitimately one of the best in the state" is better than "They serve Bloody Marys."
7. **Skip sections that aren't good enough.** Don't force the Providence section if nothing's worth the drive. Don't run a deal if you don't have a good one. Consistency of quality beats consistency of quantity.

### Typography

**Headlines:** Playfair Display (Bold/Black) — editorial, sophisticated, magazine-quality. Fallback: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif.

**Body & UI:** Source Sans 3 (Regular/SemiBold/Bold) — clean, modern, highly readable on mobile. Fallback: Arial, Helvetica, sans-serif.

**Section Labels:** Source Sans 3, 11px, Bold, ALL CAPS, 3px letter-spacing, Sandy Gold (#D4A843)

### Typography Scale
- Newsletter title: 36px / Black
- Section titles: 26px / Bold
- Event names (lineup): 18px / Bold
- Headliner event name: 24px / Bold
- Body copy: 15–17px / Regular
- Event meta (date/time): 13–14px / SemiBold
- Section labels: 11px / Bold / Uppercase / Letter-spaced
- Footer text: 12–13px / Regular

### Photography Direction

**Style:** Warm, golden-hour, candid. Real moments over posed shots. Local textures over stock photography.

**Subjects that work:**
- Sunset over Narragansett Beach
- Craft cocktails on a bar top
- Live music crowds from behind (silhouettes against stage lights)
- Weathered wood, boat hulls, beach grass, oyster shells
- Farmers market produce in morning light
- Street scenes in Wickford Village or downtown Westerly

**Subjects to avoid:**
- Generic stock beach photos (especially tropical-looking ones)
- Overly polished food photography (we're not Bon Appétit)
- Anything that could be anywhere (no sense of place)
- Group photos that look like PR shots

**Filter/Treatment:** Warm tones, slightly desaturated, natural lighting. Think "film photography" not "Instagram filter." If in doubt, lean warmer.

## Brand Applications

### Email Subject Lines
Format: `[Hook] + [Count/Promise] + [Emoji]`
- "This Week's Move: Swing Night at The Knick 💃"
- "7 Things to Do This Weekend in South County 🌊"
- "The Deal You'll Want Before Saturday 🍹"
- "Spring Just Showed Up Early — Here's Your Weekend ☀️"

### Social Media Bio
**Instagram:** "Your weekly cheat sheet for Southern RI 🌊 Events • Food • Music • Nightlife • Beach Life | Every Thursday in your inbox 👇"

**Facebook:** "The South County Signal — a free weekly newsletter curating the best things to do in Southern Rhode Island. Subscribe at southcountysignal.com."

### Brand Don'ts
- Never use the full formal name "The South County Signal" in casual copy — just "the Signal" is fine
- Never change the color palette for seasonal themes — the blue/gold is the brand year-round
- Never write in third person ("The South County Signal recommends...") — always first/second person
- Never use stock imagery that isn't recognizably Rhode Island
- Never feature a business negatively in the newsletter — if it's not good enough to recommend, just don't include it
